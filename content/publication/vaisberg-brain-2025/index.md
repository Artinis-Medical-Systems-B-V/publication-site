abstract: 'There is growing interest in using neuroimaging to understanding listening
  effort in individuals with hearing loss, with a particular focus on how innovative
  hearing aid features impact listening effort. This study used functional near infrared
  spectroscopy (fNIRS) to investigate the neural basis of listening effort in experienced
  hearing aid users. This study evaluated the impact of a new deep learning-based
  noise management hearing aid feature on cerebral blood oxygenation, with the expectation
  that it would be associated with less oxygenation in the left prefrontal cortex
  compared to a standard quiet-listening hearing aid program. Twenty-six experienced
  hearing aid users repeated sentence-final words from sentences presented in noise
  while wearing individually prescribed hearing aids in two conditions: a standard-listening
  program with an omnidirectional microphone setting and a DNN-listening program combining
  directional microphones with a deep-neural-network-based noise management algorithm.
  Listening accuracy, subjective listening effort ratings, and prefrontal oxygenation
  via fNIRS were measured throughout testing. As expected, the DNN-listening program
  was associated with higher listening accuracy, lower subjective listening effort
  ratings, and lower fNIRS-measured oxygenation in the left prefrontal cortex relative
  to the standard-listening program. The utility of fNIRS for hearing aid research
  and the interaction of listening effort and other cognitive processes are discussed
  further.'
