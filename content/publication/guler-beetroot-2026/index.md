abstract: 'The ability of nitrate (NO₃⁻)-rich beetroot juice (BJ) to improve exercise
  depends on training status. However, the mechanisms are understudied. The aim of
  this study was to investigate the impact of acute BJ supplementation on the Special
  Judo Fitness Test (SJFT) outcomes in elite and recreational judokas.  Forty judo
  athletes (17 elite and 23 recreational) completed two SJFT sessions after drinking
  either BJ or a placebo in a randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled crossover
  design. Plasma NO₃⁻ and lactate, tissue saturation index (TSI) and SJFT outcomes
  were measured. Plasma NO₃⁻ increased after BJ intake in both groups (p textless 0.001).
  Elite athletes achieved more total throws (TT) (p textless 0.05) and exhibited lower
  TSI during SJFT, 1-min post-SJFT heart rate (HR₁), and SJFT index (p textless 0.05),
  but higher post-SJFT lactate and TSI than recreational athletes (p textless 0.05).
  TT did not improve following supplementation (p textgreater 0.05). BJ increased
  post-SJFT TSI (elite: 2.6 ± 3% vs. recreational: 4.55 ± 2.6%) and reduced HR₁ (elite:
  − 5.6 ± 3.3% vs. recreational: − 9.8 ± 4.7%) and SJFT index (elite: − 2.7 ± 5.6%
  vs. recreational: − 6 ± 5.8%) overall (p textless 0.001 compared to placebo), with
  greater effects in recreational athletes (p textless 0.05). BJ also decreased post-SJFT
  lactate only in recreational athletes (p textless 0.01). Higher maximal oxygen uptake
  correlated with smaller BJ-induced improvements in lactate (r = − 0.57, p textless 0.001),
  HR₁ (r = − 0.50, p textless 0.001), SJFT index (r = − 0.43, p textless 0.01), and
  TSI during the third period of SJFT effort (r = − 0.5, p textless 0.001) and during
  1-min recovery (r = − 0.35, p textless 0.05). BJ primarily attenuates the physiological
  cost and enhances recovery-related markers during the judo performance, rather than
  directly increasing the performance, with responsiveness modulated by training status.'
